🔀 Reverse Bits — Divide & Conquer

Visualizing how swap-adjacent-blocks reverses all 32 bits in 5 steps

💡 The Core Idea

Instead of reversing bits one-by-one, we reverse in progressively smaller groups: first swap the two 16-bit halves, then within each half swap 8-bit blocks, then 4-bit, 2-bit, and finally 1-bit. After 5 rounds every bit ends up in its mirror position.

🪞 Analogy: Think of a line of 32 people. First the left 16 swap places with the right 16. Then within each group of 16, the left 8 swap with the right 8. Keep halving until every pair swaps — the line is reversed!

🎭 Why Do the Masks Look Like That?

Each mask is a repeating binary pattern that selects alternating blocks of bits. The pattern's "block size" matches the current step's group size. Let's build them from scratch.

🔑 The Golden Rule:

At each step, we want to split every group into its upper half (Mask A) and lower half (Mask B), shift them in opposite directions, then OR them together to swap.

Mask A = 1s in the upper half of each group, 0s in the lower half.
Mask B = 0s in the upper half of each group, 1s in the lower half.
→ Mask B is always the bitwise complement of Mask A!

🧱 The Building Blocks

Each mask is just a small pattern repeated across 32 bits. Here's how they're constructed:

Step 1: block size 16

11111111111111110000000000000000
One group of 32 bits. Upper 16 are 1s, lower 16 are 0s.
Mask A = 0xFFFF0000
Mask B = 0x0000FFFF

Step 2: block size 8

11111111000000001111111100000000
Groups of 16, each split into 8+8.
Repeating unit: 11111111 00000000
Mask A = 0xFF00FF00
Mask B = 0x00FF00FF

Step 3: block size 4

11110000111100001111000011110000
Groups of 8, each split into 4+4.
Repeating unit: 1111 0000 = 0xF0
Mask A = 0xF0F0F0F0
Mask B = 0x0F0F0F0F

Step 4: block size 2

110011001100…repeated
Groups of 4, each split into 2+2.
Repeating unit: 1100 = 0xC
Mask A = 0xCCCCCCCC
Mask B = 0x33333333

Step 5: block size 1

10101010…repeated
Groups of 2, each split into 1+1.
Repeating unit: 10 = 0xA
Mask A = 0xAAAAAAAA
Mask B = 0x55555555
Mask A = 1s in upper half of each group  |  Mask B = ~Mask A
The repeating unit has length = 2 × block_size, with 1s in the first half and 0s in the second half.

🧠 Why does the shift amount equal the block size?

Because each half of the group is exactly block_size bits wide. To move the upper half down to the lower position, you shift right by block_size. To move the lower half up, you shift left by block_size. They cross over perfectly — like two trains swapping tracks.

🔍 Why Does This Reverse Everything?

Think of it as 5 rounds of a tournament bracket in reverse. In a normal tournament, neighbors pair up, winners advance to groups of 4, then 8, 16, 32. Here we're running it backwards:

Step 1 reverses the order of the two 16-element halves → bits are now in the correct half.
Step 2 reverses within each 8-element block → bits are now in the correct byte.
Step 3 reverses within each 4-element block → bits are now in the correct nibble.
Step 4 reverses within each 2-element block → bits are now in the correct pair.
Step 5 reverses each adjacent pair → every bit is in its final reversed position.

After step k, each bit is guaranteed to be in the correct position for the bottom (32 / 2k) bits of its address. After all 5 steps, all 32 address bits are correct → the entire number is reversed.
